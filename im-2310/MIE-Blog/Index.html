When a line is clicked, and object falls from it and disappears once it hits the ground, and reappears back to where it fell from to fall again, repeatedly, forever. So we could assume that an object placed on the top bar takes 4 times longer to hit the ground than an object on the lowest bar. To represent the process, to blur the line of the physical and conceptual. An object of importance but little value lets us consider the materiality of the object, and the importance of the concept, which becomes part of the art itself. Through a dynamic cube with colours that shift depending on the viewer’s angle, I explore the malleability of perception and the significance of perspective in art and life. Interactive design:

I began this concept after a number of iterative attempts at generating a piece which forces the viewer to forefront the surroundings of a 3d object. Originally, I had hoped to make the viewer examine their ability to see; with an original concept of presenting artwork only visible in the peripheral of the viewers screen. Such that; by reversing our expectations and presenting the blankness of digital space, we could appreciate and witness bare the world that encloses the art.

After considering more potential concepts; I began to realise that without a screen the digital world Is inaccessible. The screen is considered gone the moment we utilise it; the invisible canvas that holds host to the great illusion of everything at all. For what is an object in 3D space, but a clever illusion of a 2D screen. A collection of thousands of pixels tied together momentarily. To view, we must employ an army of pixels; should a single pixel not fall in line, we see and we know.

The greatest illusion is shattered and it becomes an annoyance we must force ourselves to forget.

So; how to show the screen exists, and is a cluster of single cell pixels working in unison. I sought to consider the role of the RGB colour space, and built what I consider to be a representation of a single pixel; it’s role over important; it’s necessity deeply required for our viewing pleasure and yet banal and almost gross to look at. Finally for the users role in accidentally breaking the illusion. By employing techniques seen in Wabi-Sabi; the Japanese art of impermanence, I would allow the user to experience the illusion, cleverly considering it’s temporal location, and then allow them to ‘look into’ the inner workings of the RGB colour spectrum, and never look back. It should be noted, this is not true Wabi-Sabi. It shows no weathering nor objects in a utilised form. Personally, I feel a connection to Wabi-Sabi with this environment. It may however, be closer to a representation of Wabi-Sabi; the flow of time, and an object that can’t return to it’s original form.

This is the role of my work, to allow the viewer to label it with a value. Whilst the importance of the minimalist movement and it’s conceptual design lives through the project; the value is entirely up to the audience. In a sweeping stage of interactive design; the pixel is so insignificant it requires the viewers interaction in order to provide it value.

This project can also be considered to utilise gestalt psychology; a sense of the indivisible whole. The screen, in this example, is utilised as a piece of the work. Whilst many digital artworks may attempt to transport us into the world; this piece specifically does not. Instead transporting us to a world where we look through a screen, and no matter how much we may want to experience the world unhindered by the piece of glass, we must consider it.

In this project, the screen is divided horizontally and vertically based on screen dimensions of X and Y. Thus, it is the vertical of the screen; the horizontal of the screen, which designates the colour of the cube. Simply put, we must consider the role of the screen and pixels. It is an essential role of this digital world.

Once again, we see that this is a literal and figurative pushback against representation and illusionism.

Process

As can be seen here, the process for creating this was largely trial and error; The biggest issue I faced was creating using the screens X and Y coordinates, as opposed to the world maps coordinates. Using the world map coordinates resulted in perspective skewing, and less freedom for the cubes placement.

Originally, it was developed so anything above 0.0 would be green. This worked as a test, however it quickly became apparent that using this method would lead to an object that needed to be placed in an exact location; furthermore, this test showed that the colour was being generated ‘in world’, as opposed to the glitch effect of being reminded that the screen exists, and has a vertical and horizontal centre.

The next challenge I sought to face, was blending. Blending typically resulted in a murky grey colour, the level of blend was hard to finesse, and I found it was not creating the nice spectrum I had envisioned. Thus, I considered adding a fourth element of saturation, using the final corner to allow the user to increase or decrease the vibrancy of the cube. Ultimately, it was purely a stylistic choice to remove this. While including saturation did make sense from a conceptual point of view, to me, it removed the isolation of RGB. I feel that, seeing over-exposed RGB stripped bare resonated with my concept of pixel colour better. In the end, I simply altered saturation through code using a public slider. This allowed me to perfect the blend, and create an aethetically pleasing and vibrant cube.

I grappled with many issues and bugs. By trawling online posts, and watching tutorials, I found I needed to look in to how unity UI is built. After I gathered information I was ready to implement. This proceeded to generate a new set of issues and bugs, so I took my code to ChatGPT, and asked it to help me understand how to implement my code smoothly.

Ultimately, this was a challenging technical process. Considering I focussed my intents on generating a thought provoking concept; I felt it necessary to utilise all tools available and expedite my technical process as much as possible.
